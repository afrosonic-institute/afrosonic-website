THE ORGANISATION

About Afrosonic

We preserve and archive Nigerian musical heritage through community events, oral history interviews, editorial publishing, and the collection of at-risk recordings — creating spaces where this music can be heard, documented, and kept by the people who care about it most.

WHAT WE DO

Preservation is not passive.

Nigerian popular music from the 1950s to the early 2000s is at risk. Not from censorship or deliberate erasure — but from neglect. Recordings degrade. Vinyl collections get dispersed. The musicians who made this music age, and the stories they carry age with them.

Afrosonic exists to interrupt that process. We preserve and archive Nigerian musical heritage through community events, oral history interviews, editorial publishing, and the collection of at-risk recordings.

Our work is not academic. It happens in rooms full of people dancing to music their parents listened to. It happens in long conversations with musicians at their homes.

WHERE WE STARTED

From Nigerian Funk to Afrosonic.

Afrosonic began as Nigerian Funk — a personal project born from a simple frustration: growing up in Nigeria without easy access to the music that had defined Nigerian culture for decades.

Nigerian Funk became a platform. The platform became a community. And as the community grew, so did the ambition — from playlists and social media to live events, research, film screenings, and eventually a publication and a programme of oral history interviews.

In 2022, the organisation was restructured as IPAHM — the Initiative for Preservation and Archiving of Historical Music — with a formal governance structure, a board of trustees, and a mandate to take the work seriously.

The name changed. The mission didn't.
